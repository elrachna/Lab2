Temple of Preah Vihear

The Temple of Preah Vihear is an ancient Hindu temple located on the cliffs of the Dângrêk Mountains, in Cambodia, near the border with Thailand. It was primarily built during the reign of the Khmer Empire in the 9th to 12th centuries, dedicated to the deity Shiva. The temple's strategic position atop a 525-meter cliff offers breathtaking views of the surrounding plains, emphasizing its role as a religious and symbolic center. Preah Vihear is noted for its well-preserved architecture and intricate stone carvings, reflecting the height of Khmer artistic achievement. The temple complex was designed in a series of sanctuaries connected by a long, processional causeway, leading worshipers through various spiritual stages. Despite its religious origins, the site has been a source of tension between Cambodia and Thailand, with both nations claiming ownership due to its location. In 1962, the International Court of Justice ruled in favor of Cambodia, granting it sovereignty over the temple. In 2008, it was designated a UNESCO World Heritage site, cementing its status as a symbol of cultural and historical significance in Southeast Asia.
